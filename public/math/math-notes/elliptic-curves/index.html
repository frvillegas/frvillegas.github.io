    • The point is that if conjugating by

      $$U$$

      we get integral matrices then all of the polynomials must vanish modulo

      $$\det U$$

      . Since the scheme has no points over

      $${\mathbb F}_2$$

      there is, in particular, no

      $$U$$

      of determinant

      $$2$$

      over

      $$\mathbb Z$$

      that works. But there are such

      $$U$$

      ’s over any ring that surjects to

      $${\mathbb F}_4$$

      . For example, the ring can be

      $${\mathbb Z}[\zeta_3]$$

      and we can take

      $$ U:= \left( \begin{matrix} 1&\zeta_3\\ 0& 2 \end{matrix} \right) $$

      The elliptic curve

      $$E_t$$

      has Kodaira symbols

      $$IV^*, I_2, II$$

      at

      $$[0, 1, \infty]$$

      respectively.

      $$\sqrt{-3}$$

      $$A_1$$

      $$A_2$$

      Now we can make the change of variables

      $$ (x,y)\mapsto ((1-t)^{1/6}x, (1-t)^{7/12}y) $$

      and get the curve

      $$ C:\qquad y^2=x(x^6+ax^3+1), \qquad \qquad a=(2-t)/(1-t)^{1/2}. $$

      This curve has the involution

      $$ \iota:\quad (x,y)\mapsto (1/x,y/x^4). $$

      We now find an equation for the quotient curve

      $$E:=C/\langle\iota\rangle$$

      .

      Two rational functions fixed by

      $$\iota$$

      are:

      $$x_1:=x+1/x$$

      and

      $$y_1:=y(1+1/x^4)$$

      . We have

      $$ y_1^2=y^2(1+1/x^4)^2=(x^{14} + ax^{11} + 2x^{10} + x^8 + 2ax^7 + x^6 + 2x^4 + ax^3 + 1)/x^7. $$

      We write the righthand side as a polynomial

      $$h_1(x_1)$$

      and find

      $$ h_1(x)=x^7 - 7x^5 + ax^4 + 16x^3 - 4ax^2 - 12x + 4a $$

pold(pol)=

        local(z,k,f);

                       k=poldegree(pol);
                       z=polcoeff(pol,k);

                       f+=z*x^k;
